What is Loan Amortization?

Amortization is the process of spreading out a loan into a series of fixed payments over a specific period. You will encounter amortization most commonly with mortgages, auto loans, and personal loans. While your monthly payment remains exactly the same every month, the composition of that payment changes drastically over the life of the loan.

How the Amortization Schedule Works (Front-Loaded Interest)

When you look at an amortization schedule, you will notice a frustrating reality of banking: interest is front-loaded. Because interest is calculated based on your remaining principal balance, your early payments are primarily paying off interest, doing very little to reduce your actual debt.

For example, in the first year of a 30-year mortgage, up to 70% or 80% of your monthly payment goes straight to the bank as interest. As the years pass and your principal balance slowly shrinks, the interest charge decreases. By the final years of the loan, almost your entire monthly payment goes directly toward paying off the principal.

The Mathematical Formula

The calculation to determine your fixed monthly payment ($M$) is based on the principal amount ($P$), the monthly interest rate ($r$), and the total number of payments ($n$). The mathematical equation is:

$$M = P \frac{r(1+r)^n}{(1+r)^n - 1}$$

Because this formula calculates compounding interest across hundreds of periods, calculating an entire schedule by hand is incredibly tedious, which is why financial institutions and consumers rely on calculators like this one.

The Secret Power of Extra Payments

One of the most powerful features of our calculator is the "Extra Monthly Payment" field. If you add just $100 extra to your monthly mortgage payment, you aren't just paying it off a little faster—you are fundamentally altering the amortization schedule.

Any extra money you pay goes 100% directly toward the principal balance. Because the principal drops faster, the interest calculated for the following month drops as well. Paying just a fraction extra each month can shave years off your loan term and save you tens of thousands of dollars in interest over the life of the loan.

Frequently Asked Questions (FAQ)

1. Should I choose a 15-year or 30-year loan?

A 15-year loan will have significantly higher monthly payments, but you will pay drastically less in total interest over the life of the loan, and usually secure a lower interest rate. A 30-year loan offers lower, more manageable monthly payments, giving you flexibility, but you will pay a massive amount of interest to the bank over three decades. Many financial advisors recommend taking the 30-year loan for safety, but making extra payments as if it were a 15-year loan.

2. Does this calculator include taxes and insurance?

No. This calculator provides a pure amortization schedule for Principal and Interest (P&I) only. If you are calculating a mortgage, remember that your actual monthly bill from the lender will likely include property taxes, homeowners insurance, and potentially Private Mortgage Insurance (PMI) held in escrow.
